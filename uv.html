Ultraviolet

Ultraviolet (UV) light is part of a family of radiations called the electromagnetic (EM) spectrum. UV is just beyond the violet end of visible light and has smaller wavelengths and greater energy. UV radiation has enough energy to break chemical bonds to cause ionization, a process in which electrons break away from atoms. The resulting vacancy affects the chemical properties of the atoms and causes them to form or break chemical bonds that they otherwise would not. This is damaging to paintwork materials as it induce what we call photdegradation or commonly know as bleaching.

Regardless of UV intensity, some color paints are more susceptible to fading in particular to red paint which usually losing its color the fastest. That's because every color has a specific wavelength, and red paint absorbs all wavelengths except those in the red band, which are reflected. In 1993, it was discovered by the Federal Agency of Materials (Berlin) that UV rays combined with Acid/mineral rain water could damage paintwork (commonly known as rain spots/water etching) in a very short period of time. To combat this, the formula of Car-Lack68 was updated to completely protect the paintwork against UV sun rays thereby near eliminating the problems caused by acid/calcified rainwater. At that time, no other product in the car care market provides this level of protection.
